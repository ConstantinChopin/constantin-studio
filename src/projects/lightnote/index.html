Overview

Every verb the terminal knew becomes a room on the canvas.

Lightnote is Page Nineteen's creative AI toolkit — a Python engine that embeds images with CLIP, writes their tags and palettes, trains LoRA styles on fal.ai, and generates with Flux and Stability. All of it ran from the command line: precise, scriptable, invisible.

The canvas gives that engine a surface without adding a word to its vocabulary. Images gather into sets; sets feed processes; processes leave runs. Load Set, Train LoRA, Text to Image — the pipeline reads left to right, the way it always executed.

Nothing on the canvas asks to be configured. You select, and the interface answers — arrange, delete, or fold the selection into a set. The cream bar is the toolkit's one warm move, reserved for the moment of decision.

The Grammar

One operation, two registers.

The interface was not invented; it was transcribed. What the terminal writes as flags, the canvas holds as fields — the trigger word TOK travels unchanged from shell to node, and a trained style lands back in the library either way.

"Nothing here was invented. The interface was already present in the toolkit's grammar — the canvas only gave it somewhere to stand." On the design of Lightnote
